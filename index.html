About Me

I am currently a Life Sciences Research Professional in the lab of Dr. Glaivy Batsuli at Stanford School of Medicine, where I study adaptive immune dynamics using murine models and single-cell RNA sequencing.

My background spans computational modeling, in vitro, and in vivo approaches applied to signaling and learning-like processes across the adaptive immune system, gut microbiome, and neural systems. I aim to develop quantitative models — drawing on frameworks such as predictive coding, reinforcement learning, and network dynamics — to explain how biological systems encode, update, and act on information across scales.

To this end, I am aiming to pursue a PhD with a focus on computational cognitive science. I am currently investigating the geometric structure of EEG population dynamics using PCA and kernel PCA, a line of questioning I hope to pursue further in graduate school.

Immune Signaling Network Analysis in Autoimmune Disease

As part of a computational methods project, I reproduced and evaluated cell–cell communication analyses from a published multi-omics study of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and rheumatoid arthritis (RA), two autoimmune diseases with complex and incompletely understood mechanisms.

I focused on ligand–receptor–mediated signaling inferred using CellChat, examining the macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF) pathway in SLE and the Galectin-9 (GAL) pathway in RA. Using single-cell RNA sequencing data, I reconstructed intercellular communication networks and generated multiple complementary visualizations (circle plots, chord diagrams, heatmaps, and violin plots) to compare signaling structure between patient and healthy control cohorts.

My analysis aligned with the primary trends reported in the original study: reduced MIF signaling in SLE and elevated GAL signaling in RA. However, the specific inferred interactions differed in several cases, likely reflecting sensitivity to preprocessing choices (e.g., normalization) and software version differences. Interpreting these discrepancies as part of the analysis, I found that while edge-level details varied, higher-level network patterns were robust.

Microbiome–Disease Association Modeling

This project is a Python-based analytical tool designed to explore potential relationships between gut microbiome composition and disease phenotypes. By leveraging publicly available biological databases such as GMrepo, MeSH, and Ensembl, the program enables users to investigate whether specific bacterial taxa (at the genus or species level) differ in abundance between individuals with a given condition and healthy controls. The workflow is accessible via a command-line interface, where users can input one or more phenotype–microbe pairs and receive both statistical outputs and visualizations.

At its core, the project applies established statistical methods to assess these relationships. For each phenotype–bacteria pair, the program computes summary statistics including mean relative abundance and standard deviation for both disease and control groups. It then performs both a Welch’s t-test and a Mann–Whitney U test to evaluate whether differences between groups are statistically significant. To improve the validity of parametric testing, the tool optionally applies transformations (such as Box-Cox or logarithmic transformations) and generates diagnostic plots, including histograms and Q–Q plots, to help assess data distribution and normality.

The results produced by gut-brain-omics provide a quantitative framework for identifying microbiome patterns associated with neuropsychiatric conditions and, more broadly, the gut–brain axis. For example, in an analysis of Bifidobacterium abundance in individuals with depression, the tool identified significantly lower mean abundance in the disease group compared to healthy controls, with strong statistical support across both parametric and nonparametric tests. While these findings are correlational, they point to measurable biological differences that may relate to cognitive and affective processes. By integrating data retrieval, transformation, and statistical inference into a single pipeline, this project reflects a computational approach to studying complex, multilevel systems at the intersection of biology and cognition.
